About Red Okra

Red okra, known for its striking color, is a variety of the traditional green okra and belongs to the mallow family, which includes cotton, hibiscus, and hollyhock1. Native to Ethiopia, okra is the only member of this family to bear edible fruit1. The red okra, particularly the ‘Red Burgundy’ variety, was developed by Leon Robbins at Clemson University and introduced in 1983, later winning the All-America Selections award in 19881.

The plant produces 2 to 5-inch long, torpedo-shaped fruit that is completely edible2. While the red okra’s vibrant hue is its most distinguishing feature, it unfortunately loses its red color when cooked, turning green instead2. This color change is due to the presence of anthocyanins, which are natural plant pigments that react to changes in pH during cooking.



Growing red okra is similar to cultivating green okra. It requires starting the seeds indoors four to six weeks before the last frost date or directly outside two to four weeks after the last expected frost1. The seeds can be tricky to germinate, so it’s recommended to either gently crack the outer coating with nail clippers or soak them in water overnight to facilitate the process1. Once established, the plants should be spaced 6 to 8 inches apart in rich soil amended with plenty of compost, as okra is a heavy feeder1. The pods typically form within 55 to 60 days.

In the garden, red okra makes a showy specimen plant thanks to its attractive fruit and can be a conversation starter due to its unusual color. Despite the color difference, the taste of red okra is similar to that of green okra, with a mild flavor and the same mucilaginous properties that make it a popular thickener in dishes like gumbo2. The red okra can be used interchangeably with green okra in any recipe, offering a unique aesthetic to dishes before cooking.

Medical Importance of Red okra


  1. Heart Health: Red okra contains antioxidants and fiber that may contribute to heart health by managing cholesterol levels and reducing blood pressure.

  2. Blood Sugar Management: The fiber content in red okra can aid in blood sugar management, which is particularly beneficial for individuals with Type 2 diabetes.

  3. Digestive Health: he dietary fiber found in red okra helps promote better digestion and can contribute to a healthy gut.

  4. Bone Health: With its calcium and vitamin K content, red okra supports bone health, enhancing bone density and overall skeletal strength.




Detailed Description of Cooking Lotus Stem


Ingredients


    • Fresh okra pods
    • Olive oil
    • Smoked paprika
    • Ground cumin
    • Kosher salt
    • Black pepper
    • Garlic powder
    • Cayenne pepper (optional for heat)


Instructions



    1. Preparation: Begin by preheating your oven to 425°F. While the oven is heating, thoroughly wash the okra and ensure it’s completely dry. This helps to reduce the sliminess that okra is sometimes known for.
    2. Seasoning: Slice the okra pods in half lengthwise to create long, lovely shapes that are easier to eat and manage. In a bowl, toss the okra with olive oil, smoked paprika, ground cumin, kosher salt, black pepper, and garlic powder. If you like a bit of spice, a dash of cayenne pepper can be added.
    3. Roasting: Spread the seasoned okra in a single layer on a baking sheet. Place in the oven and roast until the okra is tender and the edges are browned and crispy, which should take about 20 to 25 minutes. It’s recommended to stir the okra halfway through the cooking time to ensure even roasting.


Final Preparation:
  • Serving: Once done, serve the roasted okra immediately to enjoy its best texture and flavor. It makes a great side dish to meats or can be enjoyed on its own as a healthy snack.
