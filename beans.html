About Clove Beans

Clove beans, also known as Ipomoea muricata, are a vining plant that bears clusters of fruit1. Despite their name, they are not actually beans but are more closely related to the Morning Glory family. They are popular in Kerala, where they are called Nitya vazhuthananga in Malayalam, and along the Konkan coast of India1.

The plant can grow up to 18 feet in height and produces yellow or white flowers2. The seeds have a germination rate of above 80% and the harvest typically starts around 80 days after planting2. For optimal growth, clove beans require full sunlight and should be watered once every three days. It’s recommended to apply fertilizer twice a month and use neem-based pest control to protect the plant from insects2.

Clove beans are appreciated for their unique appearance, resembling flower buds, and are often grown for both their ornamental value and their fruit3. If you’re interested in gardening or horticulture, clove beans could be a fascinating addition to your garden.

In terms of cultivation, Ipomoea muricata requires a sunny location with well-drained soil. It is a perennial vine in tropical climates but can be grown as an annual in temperate zones. The plant benefits from regular watering and fertilization, and it’s recommended to provide support for its climbing stems. Seeds or cuttings can propagate the plant, and with proper care, it can start producing fruit in about one and a half months after planting2. Clove beans have a significant cultural and historical presence in the areas where they are grown. In Kerala, the swollen peduncles are a common ingredient in traditional dishes, and the plant is valued for its continuous fruiting, hence the name Nitya vazhuthananga, which translates to everlasting brinjal’3.

Overall, clove beans offer a combination of aesthetic appeal, culinary uses, and cultural significance, making them a cherished plant in various parts of the world where they are cultivated.

Medical Importance of Clove Beans


  1. Antioxidant Properties: Clove beans contain flavonoids, which are known for their antioxidant effects1. Antioxidants help neutralize harmful free radicals in the body, which can prevent cell damage and reduce the risk of chronic diseases.

  2. Nutritional Content: The plant is a source of essential vitamins such as vitamin C and vitamin A, as well as important minerals like potassium, calcium, and iron1. These nutrients are vital for maintaining overall health and supporting bodily functions.

  3. Analgesic Effects: Traditionally, clove beans have been used to manage pain, including headaches and muscle aches, due to their analgesic properties2.

  4. Immune System Support: Compounds found in clove beans can enhance immune system function, helping the body to fight off infections2.

  5. Traditional Remedies:In various cultures, clove beans have been used to treat a range of ailments. For example, the tubers are used by local communities in Bangladesh to treat spinal cord pain, increase breast milk, body strength, and sperm count, control tuberculosis, act as a blood purifier in jaundice, and treat biliary disorders3.

  6. Folkloric Uses:In India, the raw tubers of clove beans are consumed, and sun-dried root powder is administered to regulate proper menstruation and body weight. The plant has several medicinal properties, including acting as a cholagogue, galactagogue, aphrodisiac, demulcent, and purgative4.


Detailed Description of Cooking Clove Beans


Ingredients


  • Clove beans (Nithya Vazhuthananga) - 1 plateful
  • Onion - 1
  • Shallots - 5
  • Curry leaves - 10-12
  • Dry red chillies - 2
  • Oil - 2 tsp
  • Turmeric powder - 1 tsp
  • Red chilly powder - 1-2 tsp
  • Cumin seeds – 1/2 tsp
  • Green chilies – 2
  • Urid dhal - 1 tsp
  • Mustard seeds - 1 tsp
  • Garlic cloves - 2
  • Ginger - 1 small piece
  • Desiccated or grated coconut - ½ cup


Instructions


  • Preparation:

    1. Wash the clove beans and slice them into two equal portions. To prevent discoloration, keep the cut beans in a bowl of water until cooking time.

    2. Chop the onion, shallots, green chilies, garlic, and ginger into small pieces.

    3. Heat oil in a pan. Once hot, add mustard seeds, cumin seeds, and let them splutter.

    4. Add curry leaves and dry red chili. Stir in urid dhal until it turns golden brown.

    5. Saute onions, shallots, ginger, and garlic until the raw smell disappears.

    6. Add the chopped clove beans along with salt, turmeric powder, and red chilly powder. Cook by sprinkling water occasionally. Avoid adding too much water.



Final Preparation:
  • Once the vegetables are nearly cooked, check for salt and adjust if necessary.
  • Finally, add the grated coconut and stir fry for a minute until everything is well combined.
