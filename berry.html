About Sun Berry

The sunberry, also known as wonderberry or scientifically as Solanum retroflexum, is a plant with a rich history and intriguing characteristics. Developed in the early 1900s by the famed horticulturist Luther Burbank, the sunberry is a compact, bushy plant that typically reaches a height of 12 to 24 inches1. It is known for its attractive white flowers that bloom in midsummer, which then give way to hundreds of small, dark blue-purple fruits.

The sunberry is a member of the nightshade family, which includes both poisonous and edible plants. While the green, unripe fruits of the sunberry may be toxic, the ripe berries are safe to consume1. These berries are not particularly flavorful when eaten fresh, as they can taste similar to an unripe tomato. However, when cooked and sweetened, they transform into a delicious ingredient for pies and preserves.

Cultivation of the sunberry is straightforward and akin to that of tomatoes or peppers. It is an annual plant in most climates and does not tolerate frost. Seeds are typically started indoors in late winter and transplanted outdoors after the last frost, or directly sown outdoors in warmer climates without late frosts2. The plant’s care is minimal, making it an easy addition to a home garden.

The sunberry’s unique development and the simplicity of its care have made it a subject of interest among gardening enthusiasts. Its ability to produce fruit from early summer until autumn provides a long season of harvest, making it a rewarding plant for those looking to grow something a little different in their gardens

Medical Importance of Sunberries


  1. Migraine Relief: Sunberries contain biologically active substances that have a mild analgesic effect, which can help reduce the intensity of migraine attacks and facilitate the general condition of the patient.

  2. Digestive Health: Regular consumption of sunberries is believed to aid in the normalization of the digestive tract, thanks to the fiber and other beneficial compounds found in the fruit.

  3. Hemostatic and Vasoconstrictive Action: The berry’s components are known to have hemostatic properties, meaning they can help stop bleeding. Additionally, they have a vasoconstrictive action, which can be beneficial in controlling hemorrhages.

  4. Sleep Disorders: Sunberries may also offer help with sleep disorders. Their nutritional content, including certain vitamins and minerals, might contribute to better sleep quality.



Detailed Description of Sunberries used in different dishes


Sweet Dishes with Sunberries:


      • Garden Huckleberry Preserves: A popular way to use sunberries is in preserves. The berries are cooked down with sugar to create a sweet and flavorful spread that's perfect for toast, scones, or as a filling for pastries.

      • Pies and Tarts: Sunberries make for a delicious filling in pies and tarts, similar to blueberries. They are often mixed with sugar and other fruit to create a rich and tangy dessert.

      • Syrups: Sunberry syrup can be drizzled over pancakes, waffles, or ice cream for a unique twist on traditional syrups.



    Savory Dishes with Sunberries:
    • Sunberry Salsa: Sunberries can be mixed with onions, cilantro, lime juice, and jalapeños to create a unique salsa that pairs well with grilled chicken or fish.

    • Salads: Toss sunberries into a salad with mixed greens, nuts, and a vinaigrette dressing for a refreshing summer dish.

    • Meat Glazes: A sunberry glaze can add a sweet and tangy flavor to pork or beef roasts.
